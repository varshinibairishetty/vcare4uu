Common Mental Health Disorders

Mental health disorders are common, affecting many individuals worldwide. Understanding these conditions is vital for proper treatment and support. Here are some common disorders:

Depression

+

Depression is a serious mood disorder causing symptoms that affect how a person feels, thinks, and handles daily activities.

  • Persistent sadness or low mood
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in activities
  • Changes in appetite or weight
  • Sleep disturbances (insomnia or oversleeping)
  • Fatigue or loss of energy
  • Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
  • Difficulties concentrating or making decisions
  • Restlessness or slowed movements
  • Thoughts of death or suicide
  • Physical symptoms (e.g., aches, pains) without a clear cause

Anxiety Disorders

+

  • Excessive worry or anxiety about various aspects of life
  • Restlessness or feeling on edge
  • Fatigue or difficulty concentrating
  • Irritability
  • Muscle tension
  • Sleep disturbances (difficulty falling or staying asleep)
  • Rapid heartbeat or palpitations
  • Shortness of breath or feeling smothered
  • Feeling dizzy, lightheaded, or faint
  • Avoidance of certain situations or places

Eating Disorders

+

Eating disorders involve extreme emotions, attitudes, and behaviors related to weight and food.

  • Extreme concern with body weight or shape
  • Severe restriction of food intake
  • Binge eating followed by purging
  • Skipping meals or avoiding certain food groups
  • Changes in appetite or eating habits
  • Excessive exercise
  • Preoccupation with food, dieting, or body image
  • Withdrawal from social activities involving food
  • Physical symptoms such as dizziness, fatigue, or digestive issues
  • Emotional symptoms like anxiety or irritability around meal times

Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder (OCD)

+

OCD is characterized by uncontrollable, reoccurring thoughts and behaviors that the person feels compelled to repeat.

  • Intrusive and unwanted thoughts (obsessions)
  • Compulsive behaviors or rituals to alleviate anxiety (compulsions)
  • Excessive concern about dirt, germs, or contamination
  • Need for symmetry or orderliness
  • Fear of harming oneself or others
  • Checking behaviors (e.g., repeatedly checking locks or appliances)
  • Repeating actions (e.g., counting, tapping) a specific number of times
  • Difficulty discarding items, regardless of their value
  • Spending significant time on rituals that interfere with daily life
  • Emotional distress when unable to perform compulsive behaviors

Phobia

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

    • Intense fear or anxiety about a specific object or situation
    • Avoidance of the feared object or situation
    • Physical symptoms such as sweating, trembling, or increased heart rate
    • Feeling faint, dizzy, or lightheaded when confronted with the phobia
    • Difficulty breathing or a sensation of choking
    • Nausea or stomach discomfort
    • Panic attacks when exposed to the phobia
    • Feeling a loss of control or a sense of impending doom
    • Overwhelming desire to escape the situation
    • Distress that is out of proportion to the actual danger posed

Schizophrenia

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Delusions
  • Hallucinations
  • Disorganized thinking
  • Disorganized or abnormal motor behavior
  • Negative symptoms (e.g., lack of motivation, reduced emotional expression)
  • Difficulty with concentration and attention
  • Social withdrawal
  • Difficulty in distinguishing between reality and imagination
  • Emotional flatness
  • Difficulty in performing daily tasks

ADHD

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Difficulties with attention and focus
  • Difficulty organizing tasks and activities
  • Frequent forgetfulness in daily activities
  • Impulsivity or acting without thinking
  • Restlessness or fidgeting
  • Difficulty staying seated in situations where it is expected
  • Talking excessively or interrupting others
  • Difficulty following instructions and completing tasks
  • Frequent daydreaming or distraction
  • Difficulty regulating emotions

Bipolar Disorder

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Extreme mood swings, including manic and depressive episodes
  • Increased energy or activity levels during manic phases
  • Decreased need for sleep during manic episodes
  • Racing thoughts or rapid speech
  • Impulsivity or engaging in risky behaviors during mania
  • Feelings of euphoria or heightened self-esteem during manic phases
  • Persistent sadness or hopelessness during depressive episodes
  • Loss of interest in activities once enjoyed
  • Difficulty concentrating or making decisions
  • Changes in appetite or weight
  • Thoughts of death or suicide during depressive episodes

Dementia

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Memory loss that disrupts daily life
  • Difficulty communicating or finding words
  • Difficulty with reasoning or problem-solving
  • Difficulty performing familiar tasks
  • Disorientation to time and place
  • Changes in mood and personality
  • Loss of initiative or withdrawal from social activities
  • Misplacing items or losing the ability to retrace steps
  • Difficulty understanding visual images and spatial relationships
  • Behavioral changes, such as agitation or aggression

Learning Disorders

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Difficulties with reading (dyslexia)
  • Difficulties with writing (dysgraphia)
  • Difficulties with math (dyscalculia)
  • Poor academic performance compared to peers
  • Difficulty understanding and following instructions
  • Struggles with memorizing information
  • Difficulty organizing tasks and materials
  • Problems with time management
  • Low self-esteem related to academic performance
  • Frustration or anxiety regarding schoolwork

Dissociative Identity Disorder(DID)

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Presence of two or more distinct personality states or identities
  • Recurrent gaps in memory regarding daily events, personal information, or traumatic events
  • Feeling detached from oneâ€™s thoughts, feelings, or sense of identity
  • Disruptions in the continuity of identity and sense of self
  • Significant distress or impairment in social, occupational, or other areas of functioning
  • Experiencing a sense of unreality regarding oneself or the environment
  • Alterations in behavior, memory, and perception that are not attributable to a medical condition
  • Difficulty with emotional regulation
  • Unexplained physical symptoms or conditions
  • History of trauma, particularly during childhood

Autistic Disorder

+

A phobia is an irrational fear of a specific object, situation, or activity that is unlikely to cause harm.

  • Difficulties with social interactions and communication
  • Challenges in understanding social cues and nonverbal communication
  • Repetitive behaviors or routines
  • Intense focus on specific interests or topics
  • Sensitivity to sensory inputs (e.g., lights, sounds, textures)
  • Difficulty adapting to changes in routine or environment
  • Struggles with forming and maintaining relationships
  • Delayed language development or unusual speech patterns
  • Limited use of gestures or facial expressions
  • Difficulty with imaginative play or pretend activities
